→ Abstract

Economic policies not only affect welfare in a society but also freedom. In this paper, we propose a formal framework that allows analysing the effects of economic policies on individual freedom. We do so by delineating a class of ordinal freedom rankings on sets general enough that it can be specified to encompass different understandings of freedom in the philosophical literature. We show that these rankings have a functional representation by the means of a measure defined over classes of sets. The functional representation of the ordinal ranking allows the employment of freedom rankings in economic analysis. Our representation result relies on a so far neglected formal equivalence between qualitative probabilities in decision theory and freedom rankings. We exemplify the relevance of a functional representation of a freedom measure by drawing on a stylised example of a universal basic income policy and its effects on freedom. We show that, contrary to what has been argued, a tax scheme subsidising universal basic income does not always maximise the real freedom of the worst-off in a society.
